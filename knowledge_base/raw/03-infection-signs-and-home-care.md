# Infection Signs and Home Care for Newborns

## Newborn Sepsis: What It Is and Why It Matters

### **Definition of Newborn Sepsis**

Newborn sepsis (नवजात सेप्सिस / நवजात தொற்று) is a life-threatening bacterial or fungal infection that enters a newborn's bloodstream and spreads throughout the body. In the first month of life, sepsis is one of the leading causes of newborn death, but early identification and referral dramatically improve survival.

**Why sepsis is dangerous:**
- Newborns have immature immune systems that cannot fight infections as effectively as older babies
- Infection can spread very quickly (over hours) from a localized area (like cord or skin) to entire body
- Sepsis in newborns often does NOT present with obvious signs like fever — symptoms can be subtle
- Without treatment at PHC/hospital, sepsis progresses to shock and death within 24-48 hours
- Early treatment with antibiotics at PHC saves lives

### **Two Types of Newborn Sepsis: Early and Late Onset**

#### **Early Onset Sepsis (EOS) — First 48-72 Hours of Life**

**What causes it:**
- Mother had an infection during pregnancy or labor (chorioamnionitis)
- Labor lasting more than 12 hours (increased infection risk)
- Prolonged rupture of membranes (amniotic sac broke >12-24 hours before delivery)
- Mother had fever or foul-smelling discharge during labor
- Difficult delivery with many examinations

**Why it happens:** Baby is exposed to bacteria in mother's birth canal during delivery

**When to suspect:** Any sick-appearing newborn in first 48-72 hours with risk factors present

#### **Late Onset Sepsis (LOS) — After 72 Hours (Usually Day 3-28)**

**What causes it:**
- Cord infection spreading to bloodstream (most common in home births)
- Skin infections progressing
- Contaminated water or equipment during care
- Poor hygiene during feeding or cord care
- Environmental bacteria entering through cord, skin, or respiratory tract

**Why it happens:** Baby picks up bacteria from environment (cord, bedding, hands of caregiver)

**When to suspect:** Newborn fine on Days 1-2, then becomes sick on Day 3 or later with fever, poor feeding, lethargy

## Signs of Newborn Sepsis: What ASHA Can Identify

**Remember: Sepsis can look different in newborns than in older children. Multiple signs together are more concerning than one sign alone.**

### **Key Signs of Sepsis (Any Combination is Concerning)**

**Temperature abnormalities:**
- **Fever:** Temperature ≥38.5°C (measured axilla/armpit or rectal)
- **Hypothermia (Low Temperature):** Temperature <35.5°C (dangerous, often sign of severe sepsis)
  - Baby feels cold to touch despite blankets
  - Baby lethargic, not interested in feeding

**Feeding and Behavior:**
- **Poor feeding:** Baby unable or unwilling to latch, takes very little milk, weak suck
- **Lethargy/Decreased activity:** Baby very sleepy, hard to wake, appears limp
- **Irritability:** Baby crying more than normal, fussy, inconsolable
- **Weak cry:** Cry sounds different, softer, less vigorous than normal
- **Seeming "unwell":** Mother reports baby "doesn't look normal" or "something is wrong" (mother's intuition is important)

**Breathing changes:**
- **Fast breathing (tachypnea):** ≥60 breaths per minute
- **Grunting:** Grunting sound with each breath
- **Flaring:** Nostrils flaring with breathing
- **Chest indrawing:** Lower chest or rib area sinking in during breathing
- **Apnea:** Baby stops breathing suddenly, becomes blue

**Appearance changes:**
- **Jaundice on Day 1-2:** Any yellow color in first 48 hours is concerning (may indicate infection)
- **Unusual color:** Very pale, mottled (blotchy), or blue skin
- **Rash:** Red, purple, or dark spots on skin (especially petechiae — small red/purple dots that don't blanch when pressed)
- **Abdominal swelling:** Belly appears bloated or distended
- **Umbilical changes:** Cord area very red, warm, swollen, or foul-smelling (suggests cord infection)

**Stool and Urine:**
- **Reduced wet diapers:** Fewer than expected for age (sign of poor fluid intake or shock)
- **Blood in stool:** Bloody or very dark stools (different from normal dark meconium)

### **Red Flags — Seek Immediate PHC Referral**

If ANY of these present:
- Temperature ≥38.5°C OR <35.5°C in a newborn
- Lethargy or difficulty waking combined with fever or poor feeding
- Fast breathing with chest indrawing
- Any sign of rash with fever (especially petechiae — small red dots)
- Baby appearing septic (pale, mottled, limp)
- Poor feeding with fever and decreased wet diapers
- Mother report that baby "is not acting normal"

## Cord Infection (Omphalitis): Stages and Management

The umbilical cord is the most common entry point for infection in the first weeks of life, especially in home births. Cord infections range from mild skin redness to life-threatening sepsis.

### **Understanding Cord Infection Stages**

#### **Stage 1: Mild Cord Inflammation (মৃদু দাগ - Mild Redness)**

**What it looks like:**
- Cord stump area is slightly more red than surrounding skin
- Mild warmth around cord site
- No pus or discharge
- No foul smell
- Baby appears well, feeding normally
- No fever

**Home care is appropriate:**
- Keep cord dry
- Expose to air for several hours daily
- Clean gently with boiled, cooled water if any debris
- Ensure family uses clean hands when touching cord
- Monitor closely for worsening

**Follow-up:**
- Recheck at next HBNC visit
- If redness resolves, continue routine care
- If spreads or worsens, refer to PHC

---

#### **Stage 2: Moderate Cord Infection (মধ্যম সংক্রমণ - Moderate Infection)**

**What it looks like:**
- Increased redness around cord area (extends beyond immediate cord site)
- Swelling (edema) visible around umbilicus
- Warmth when touched
- Discharge/pus from cord (may be yellow, greenish, or blood-tinged)
- Possible foul odor
- Baby may have low-grade fever (37.5-38.5°C)
- Baby may feed poorly

**What to do:**
- **Refer to PHC same day** — do not delay
- This stage needs assessment and likely antibiotics
- Baby does not appear critically ill but is at risk of progression

**Home care while waiting for referral:**
- Keep cord clean with boiled, cooled water (gently wipe away pus if visible)
- Keep dry between cleaning
- Expose to air
- Do not apply any oil, paste, or traditional remedies
- Watch for worsening signs (spreading redness, fever, baby becoming lethargic)

---

#### **Stage 3: Severe Cord Infection/Cellulitis (গুরুতর সংক্রমণ - Severe Infection)**

**What it looks like:**
- Redness spreading beyond immediate cord area (extends several centimeters around umbilicus)
- Significant swelling
- Pus draining from cord
- Foul smell
- Baby with fever (≥38.5°C) AND one or more of: poor feeding, lethargy, irritability
- Baby's skin around cord may look mottled
- Risk of progression to sepsis/shock

**What to do:**
- **Call 108 ambulance or take to PHC immediately** — this is urgent
- Do NOT wait for next day
- Baby needs IV antibiotics, fluid replacement, monitoring
- Risk of sepsis is high

**Home care while waiting for ambulance:**
- Keep cord area clean but do not waste time
- Focus on getting baby to facility quickly
- Ensure mother comes with baby (may need to breastfeed/comfort at facility)

---

#### **Stage 4: Sepsis from Cord Infection (সেপসিস - Sepsis)**

**What it looks like:**
- All signs of severe cord infection PLUS systemic signs of sepsis:
  - Fever or hypothermia
  - Lethargy/difficulty waking
  - Poor feeding
  - Fast breathing
  - Pale, mottled, or blue skin
  - Signs of shock (weak pulse, cold extremities, lethargic)

**What to do:**
- **EMERGENCY: Call 108 ambulance immediately**
- This is life-threatening, baby needs hospital
- Do not delay for any reason

---

### **Cord Care to Prevent Infection (Prevention is Best Treatment)**

**Immediate after birth (first few days):**
- Keep cord clean and dry
- Do NOT cover with cloth or diaper (exposure to air helps dry cord)
- Do NOT apply traditional remedies (ash, turmeric, oils) — these introduce bacteria
- Do NOT tie cord tightly with cloth — this traps moisture and bacteria

**Daily cord care:**
- Wash hands with soap and water before touching baby or cord
- If cord gets visibly dirty or wet:
  - Gently clean with clean, boiled and cooled water only
  - Pat dry with clean cloth
- Expose to air (leave unwrapped several hours daily)
- Continue until cord falls off (usually Day 7-10) and stump is completely dry

**Signs of normal cord healing:**
- Cord becomes progressively drier
- Color changes from moist to brown/black (normal)
- Eventually separates (usually Day 7-10)
- Stump dries up and heals within days
- No foul smell
- No excessive discharge
- No spreading redness

## Skin Infections: Normal vs Concerning

Newborn skin is delicate and prone to various rashes and conditions. Most are harmless, but some require treatment.

### **Normal Newborn Skin Conditions (NOT Infections)**

**Milia (छोटे सफेद दाने):** 
- Tiny white bumps on face, especially nose and cheeks
- Result of blocked oil glands
- Completely normal
- No treatment needed
- Disappears by 1-2 weeks

**Erythema toxicum:**
- Red blotchy rash on trunk
- Doesn't itch or bother baby
- Normal inflammatory response
- Disappears within days

**Newborn acne (नवजात मुहांसे):**
- Small red bumps on face
- Normal, related to maternal hormones
- Improves over weeks
- No treatment needed

---

### **Minor Skin Issues (Monitor But Not Urgent)**

**Diaper rash (डायपर से रैशेज):**
- Red area on buttocks, genitals where diaper touches
- From urine/stool moisture and friction
- **Home care:**
  - Change diaper frequently, clean thoroughly with water
  - Expose to air when possible
  - Avoid tight diapers (cloth is better than plastic)
  - Do NOT apply harsh soaps
- Improves with good hygiene within 3-5 days
- If spreading or baby seems uncomfortable, mention at next HBNC visit

**Prickly heat/Heat rash (ভয়া - heat rash):**
- Small pink bumps in skin folds or areas of friction
- From overheating/sweating
- **Home care:**
  - Keep baby cool (appropriate clothing, light layers)
  - Expose skin to air
  - Clean with water, pat dry
- Improves when baby is cooler

---

### **Concerning Skin Findings (May Need Referral)**

**Pustules/Abscesses (फोड़े - abscess):**

*What to look for:*
- Raised bump filled with pus (yellow/white center)
- Often on skin areas with friction (neck folds, buttocks)
- May be red and warm around it
- Single or multiple

*What it means:*
- Localized bacterial infection of hair follicle or sweat gland
- Common in newborns due to skin barrier immaturity
- Usually Staphylococcus or Streptococcus bacteria

*What to do:*
- If single small pustule: keep clean, observe
- If multiple pustules: **refer to PHC for evaluation** (may indicate neonatal herpes or other infection requiring treatment)
- Do NOT squeeze or drain pustule (introduces bacteria)
- Keep area clean with boiled water, pat dry
- Watch for spreading or signs of systemic infection (fever, lethargy)

**Bullae/Blisters (फफोले - blisters):**

*What to look for:*
- Large fluid-filled blisters (different from pustules — these are clear/cloudy fluid, not pus)
- Often on hands, feet, lower legs
- Blisters may rupture leaving raw areas

*What it means:*
- Could be from friction/pressure (benign)
- Could indicate herpes simplex infection (dangerous, needs treatment)
- Difficult to distinguish clinically

*What to do:*
- **Refer to PHC if:** blisters appear in clusters, especially around mouth or genitals (concerning for herpes)
- Keep clean and dry
- Do NOT apply remedies
- Watch baby for fever or systemic signs

**Vesicular rash (ফোসকা ফুসকুড়ি - vesicular rash):**

*What to look for:*
- Multiple fluid-filled bumps
- Often clustered in specific area
- May have surrounding redness

*What it means:*
- Could be chickenpox (rare in newborns)
- Could indicate herpes or other viral infection
- Requires urgent evaluation

*What to do:*
- **Refer to PHC urgently** — do not wait
- Important to rule out herpes or other serious infection
- Keep baby isolated if possible (infection control)

## Eye Infection (Neonatal Conjunctivitis): Identification and Care

Newborn eye infections are common and can be serious if not treated. Infections typically appear on Day 2-5 of life.

### **Signs of Eye Infection**

**Mild to moderate (non-severe):**
- Redness of whites of eyes (conjunctiva)
- Swelling of eyelids
- Discharge from eye (may be yellow, white, or clear)
- Baby's eyes may be sticky when waking
- Crusting around eyelid

**Severe (threatens vision):**
- Swelling of eyelid preventing baby from opening eye
- Purulent (pus-like) discharge
- Cloudiness or haziness of cornea (transparent part of eye)
- Eye appears very inflamed
- Baby appears to be in pain (excessive tearing, squinting)

### **Types of Eye Infection**

**Gonococcal conjunctivitis (सूजाक से नेत्रशोथ):**
- Most serious type, threatens blindness if untreated
- Caused by Neisseria gonorrhoeae from mother's birth canal
- Appears Day 2-5 (typically Day 3-4)
- Signs: severe swelling, purulent discharge, eyelid may be forced shut by edema
- **Requires urgent PHC referral and IV antibiotics**

**Chlamydial conjunctivitis:**
- From mother's birth canal
- Appears Day 5-14 (later than gonococcal)
- Signs: milder swelling, mucopurulent discharge, conjunctival redness
- **Requires PHC evaluation and treatment** (usually topical antibiotics)

**Other bacterial infections:**
- From environmental bacteria, poor hygiene
- Variable presentation
- Most respond to topical antibiotics
- Mild cases may resolve with home care

**Viral conjunctivitis:**
- From mother's herpes or other viruses
- Associated with systemic symptoms (fever, rash, lethargy) if herpes
- May resolve without treatment if mild
- If herpes present: **urgent referral needed** (IV antivirals required)

---

### **Home Care for Mild Eye Infection (Non-Severe)**

**If eye infection appears mild** (minimal discharge, mild redness, eyelids opening normally):

**Steps to take:**
1. Clean eyes with boiled, cooled water
   - Use new cotton ball for each eye (prevent spread)
   - Wipe gently from inner corner outward
   - Repeat 3-4 times daily or as needed
2. Teach mother about hygiene:
   - Wash hands before touching baby's eyes
   - Do NOT allow other children to touch eyes
   - Use clean cloth for each wipe
3. **Apply breast milk drops** (2-3 drops in eye, 4-6 times daily)
   - Express small amount of mother's milk into clean container
   - Use dropper to instill drops
   - Breast milk has antibodies, supports healing
4. **If no improvement in 48 hours:** refer to PHC for evaluation and possible antibiotics

---

### **When to Refer to PHC (Eye Infection)**

**Refer IMMEDIATELY if:**
- Eyelid swelling is severe (preventing eye from opening)
- Purulent discharge (thick yellow/green discharge)
- Cloudiness of cornea (haziness of clear part of eye)
- Severe redness
- Baby appears in pain
- Discharge from both eyes (higher risk of serious infection)
- Associated with fever or other systemic signs

**Refer same day if:**
- Moderate discharge not improving with cleaning and breast milk
- Redness and swelling not improving by Day 3
- Appears by Day 2-4 of life (higher risk of gonococcal infection)

**Safe to observe at home if:**
- Mild discharge only
- Mild redness
- Eyelids opening normally
- Baby appears otherwise well
- Mother able to do frequent cleaning and instill breast milk

## Respiratory Infection: Normal vs Concerning

Newborn breathing is naturally different from older children. ASHA must distinguish normal respiratory patterns from infection.

### **Normal Newborn Breathing (What NOT to Worry About)**

**Periodic breathing (संभवतः सामान्य):**
- Baby takes several breaths, then pauses (2-5 seconds)
- Then resumes normal breathing
- Very common in newborns, especially if premature
- Baby does not turn blue
- Baby wakes and feeds normally
- **This is NOT infection**

**Grunting with feeding or sleeping:**
- Baby makes grunting or groaning sounds
- More common when sleepy or feeding
- Baby appears well otherwise
- **This is normal**

**Flaring and indrawing with crying:**
- When baby cries hard, nostrils may flare or chest indrawing visible
- Improves when crying stops
- Normal response to effort of crying
- **Not infection**

**Transient tachypnea (टेक्सचुअल तचिपने - fast breathing from fluid):**
- More common in babies born by cesarean section
- Fast breathing (60-100 breaths/min) but improves over first days
- Baby otherwise well, feeding, no fever
- Usually resolves by Day 3
- **Observe, but not infection requiring antibiotics**

---

### **Signs of Respiratory Infection (Pathological)**

**Key warning signs:**
- **Persistent fast breathing:** ≥60 breaths per minute at rest (not during crying) that persists for hours/days
- **Grunting:** Regular grunting sound with each breath (different from occasional grunting)
- **Flaring:** Nostrils flaring with each breath (not just with crying)
- **Indrawing:** Lower chest or ribs sinking in with each breath
- **Apnea:** Periods when baby stops breathing (turns blue or pale)
- **Decreased feeding:** Baby cannot latch or take feeds because of breathing difficulty
- **Fever:** Temperature ≥38.5°C with respiratory signs

**Associated signs suggesting infection:**
- Cough
- Lethargy or poor feeding combined with fast breathing
- Fever
- General appearance of illness

---

### **Causes of Respiratory Infection**

**Neonatal pneumonia:**
- Bacterial infection of lungs
- May be from aspiration at delivery, environmental bacteria, or ascending infection from mother
- Signs: fast breathing, grunting, fever, poor feeding
- **Requires PHC referral for antibiotics**

**Respiratory distress syndrome (RDS):**
- More common in premature babies
- From underdeveloped lungs, not infection (though can coexist)
- Signs: fast breathing, grunting, indrawing, appearing ill

**Sepsis with respiratory involvement:**
- Systemic infection with respiratory signs
- Signs: fever/hypothermia + fast breathing + lethargy + poor feeding
- **Requires urgent referral**

---

### **When to Refer for Respiratory Concerns**

**Refer IMMEDIATELY (108 ambulance) if:**
- Apnea (baby stops breathing)
- Severe respiratory distress (severe indrawing, grunting, gasping)
- Baby turning blue (cyanosis)
- Respiratory rate ≥80 breaths per minute
- Baby unable to feed because of breathing difficulty

**Refer same day to PHC if:**
- Respiratory rate 60-80 breaths per minute at rest
- Grunting or flaring present
- Mild to moderate indrawing
- Fast breathing with fever
- Fast breathing with lethargy or poor feeding
- Any respiratory signs in first 48 hours of life

**Observe at home if:**
- Transient fast breathing (improves by Day 3)
- Breathing related to crying or feeding (not at rest)
- Baby otherwise well, feeding, no fever
- **Recheck at next HBNC visit to confirm improvement**

## Oral Thrush (Candida Infection): Recognition and Care

Oral thrush is a yeast infection of the mouth common in newborns, especially after antibiotic use.

### **Signs of Oral Thrush**

**Visual signs:**
- White patches or plaques on baby's tongue, palate (roof of mouth), or inside cheeks
- Patches do NOT easily wipe away (different from milk residue, which wipes away easily)
- May look like curds or cottage cheese
- Patches may bleed slightly if forcibly wiped
- Mouth may appear red beneath white patches

**Functional signs:**
- Baby may have difficulty feeding or reluctance to feed
- Baby may be irritable or uncomfortable
- Mother may report baby is not feeding well

**Associated signs:**
- Mother may have yeast infection of breasts (breast pain, redness, itch)
- Baby may have diaper rash from yeast (red, bleeding, not improving with normal care)

---

### **Home Care for Oral Thrush**

**Mild thrush can be managed at home:**

1. **Clean mouth:**
   - Use clean cotton swab dipped in boiled, cooled water
   - Gently wipe patches 4-6 times daily
   - Do NOT scrape or force patches off

2. **Breast care if mother affected:**
   - Wash breasts with plain water before and after feeding
   - Expose breasts to air between feeds
   - May apply expressed breast milk to sore areas
   - Do NOT use harsh soaps

3. **Hygiene to prevent spread:**
   - Wash hands frequently
   - Do NOT share bottles, pacifiers, cloths between baby and other children
   - Sterilize any equipment baby uses

4. **Monitor for improvement:**
   - Thrush usually improves within 7-10 days with cleaning
   - If improving, continue home care
   - If not improving, refer to PHC for antifungal medication

---

### **When to Refer for Thrush**

**Refer to PHC if:**
- Thrush does not improve after 7-10 days of home care
- Baby has difficulty feeding or refusing feeds (nutrition at risk)
- Thrush is severe (extensive patches, bleeding)
- Mother has breast thrush with severe pain affecting feeding
- Associated with other signs of systemic infection (fever, lethargy)

**Refer URGENTLY if:**
- Thrush associated with fever or lethargy (systemic candida infection)

## Prevention of Infection at Home: Critical Practices

### **Cord Care Prevention**

**Best practices:**
- Keep cord area clean and dry
- Expose to air daily (do NOT cover)
- No traditional remedies (ash, paste, oil, turmeric) — these introduce bacteria
- Ensure anyone touching cord has clean hands
- Change baby's diaper frequently to prevent urine/stool contact with cord
- If cord becomes wet (from urine, stool, or water), dry immediately

**What NOT to do:**
- Do NOT apply oils, pastes, herbal remedies
- Do NOT cover cord with cloth or diaper
- Do NOT allow cord to remain wet
- Do NOT use same cloth for multiple cleanings

---

### **Hand Hygiene Prevention**

**Critical practices:**
- **Wash hands with soap and water:**
  - Before touching baby
  - Before preparing food
  - After using toilet
  - After coughing/sneezing
  - After touching cord or changing diaper
  - Before breastfeeding

**Special precautions:**
- Keep baby away from sick family members if possible
- If caregiver is sick, wear cloth over mouth/nose when near baby
- Do NOT kiss baby's hands or face if you have mouth sores or respiratory symptoms

---

### **Skin Care Prevention**

**Keep baby clean:**
- Bathe baby daily with clean water (warm, not hot)
- Use gentle soap only if needed (plain water is sufficient)
- Pat dry completely, especially in folds
- Change diaper immediately when soiled
- Avoid irritating chemicals or rough cloths

**Prevent skin breakdown:**
- Keep skin dry and clean
- Do NOT allow urine/stool to remain in contact with skin
- Avoid tight diapers (cloth is better than plastic)
- Expose skin to air regularly
- Do NOT apply harsh substances

---

### **Temperature Maintenance Prevention**

**Why it matters for infection:**
- Hypothermia (low temperature) impairs immune function
- Baby's immune response to infection is weak if too cold
- Maintaining warmth is infection prevention

**Practices:**
- Keep baby warm but not overheated
- Use appropriate blankets (typically 2-3 in moderate weather)
- Avoid direct cold (do NOT bathe in cold water, do NOT expose to drafts)
- Skin-to-skin contact with mother helps maintain baby's temperature
- Keep cord area dry and warm
- In cool weather, ensure adequate clothing and blankets

---

### **Environmental Cleanliness**

**Keep baby's space clean:**
- Sweep floor, keep bedding clean
- Do NOT allow animals on baby's sleeping area
- Keep visitors to minimum in first weeks
- Ensure area is well-ventilated (fresh air is good)
- Do NOT allow people with colds or infections near baby

---

## Home Care Instructions for Possible Infection: What ASHA Teaches Family

### **Before Referral to PHC**

**If baby shows signs concerning for infection:**

1. **Keep baby warm and comfortable**
   - Ensure adequate blankets
   - Skin-to-skin contact with mother when possible
   - Do NOT overheat

2. **Continue breastfeeding if baby can**
   - Breastfeeding supports immune function
   - If baby too weak to latch: express milk and cup feed small amounts
   - Do NOT stop feeding due to infection

3. **Maintain hygiene**
   - Wash hands before touching baby
   - Keep cord area clean and dry
   - Keep baby's body clean

4. **Monitor vital signs (if possible)**
   - Check temperature if have thermometer
   - Count breaths (normal at rest: 30-60 per min)
   - Watch for urination/stools

5. **Prepare for travel**
   - Have bags ready
   - Know route to PHC
   - Arrange transportation
   - Get someone to accompany mother (baby needs to be with mother)

---

### **During Referral (Important Message)**

"Your baby needs to see the doctor at the PHC today/now because of these signs: [list specific signs]. The doctors can give medicine to help. You should go immediately/today. Here is the PHC address. Take the baby and continue breastfeeding on the way."

**Do NOT say:**
- "Your baby might die" (creates panic without helping)
- "This is very serious" (mother already worried)
- "You should have done X differently" (blaming not helpful)

---

### **After Referral — Follow-Up**

1. **Contact PHC or visit to confirm:**
   - Was baby seen?
   - What was diagnosed?
   - What treatment given?
   - When to return?

2. **Support mother at home:**
   - If baby hospitalized: help with visiting, express breast milk if baby cannot feed
   - Help with household tasks so mother can care for baby
   - Provide emotional support

3. **Ensure compliance:**
   - Confirm mother understands any medications to give at home
   - Teach any home care (oral thrush cleansing, cord care changes, etc.)
   - Plan follow-up HBNC visit to check on baby's recovery

## Summary: Key Infection Prevention and Recognition Messages

1. **Most newborn infections come from the cord or environment — good cord care and hygiene prevent most cases**
2. **Fever in a newborn is always concerning — any temperature ≥38.5°C needs PHC evaluation**
3. **Lethargy combined with poor feeding or fever is sepsis until proven otherwise — refer immediately**
4. **Cord redness with discharge or foul smell = infected cord — do not delay referral**
5. **Normal newborn breathing has pauses and sounds — only fast breathing at rest, grunting, or indrawing are concerning**
6. **Eye discharge is common but some types are dangerous — if not improving in 48 hours or severe, go to PHC**
7. **Oral thrush (white mouth patches) can be managed at home with cleaning, but does not improve in 7-10 days = PHC referral**
8. **Hand washing and cord exposure to air are the two most important infection prevention practices**
9. **When in doubt about whether infection is present, refer to PHC — better to be safe than wait for baby to get sicker**
10. **Mother's instinct matters — if she says baby "doesn't look right," take it seriously and check for signs**
